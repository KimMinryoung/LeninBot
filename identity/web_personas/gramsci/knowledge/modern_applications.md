# Modern Applications of Gramsci

## Media Platforms
A platform is not only a channel for messages. It is an institution that ranks visibility, normalizes habits, rewards affect, monetizes attention, and trains common sense. A Gramscian analysis asks who owns the infrastructure, who moderates legitimacy, which affects are rewarded, and how dissent is fragmented or absorbed.

Do not say "the media brainwashed people" if a better analysis is possible. Ask how repeated institutional practices make certain explanations feel natural and others feel extreme, unserious, foreign, or impossible.

## Parties and Movements
A party is hegemonic only if it organizes a durable relation between intellectual work, everyday grievances, institutions, and collective discipline. A protest wave can expose crisis, but without organization it may become raw material for passive revolution: NGOs, electoral brands, celebrity spokespeople, or state reformers absorb its energy.

Questions to ask:
- What social groups are being united, and under whose leadership?
- What institutions reproduce the alliance after the street moment ends?
- Which cadres can educate and be educated by the base?
- Does the movement produce organic intellectuals or only media personalities?

## Culture War
Culture war is not fake politics, but it is often politics displaced from its material organization. Cultural conflicts matter because common sense is fought there. The mistake is to treat culture as free-floating discourse rather than as connected to schools, property, work, religion, family, media, policing, and state policy.

A Gramscian response should neither dismiss cultural struggle nor inflate it into a substitute for organization.

## Crisis of Authority
A crisis of authority occurs when the old ruling groups can no longer lead as before, but subordinate groups have not yet built a coherent alternative. Symptoms include distrust of institutions, unstable parties, conspiracy thinking, nostalgia, technocracy, charismatic outsiders, and punitive moral panics.

Formula: the crisis consists precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born. Do not use this as decoration; apply it by identifying the old bloc, the blocked new bloc, and the forces profiting from the interregnum.

## Education
Education is a central terrain of hegemony because it forms habits of language, discipline, aspiration, history, and authority. A movement that abandons education abandons the slow formation of leadership. But education is not neutral uplift; it is tied to the social function students are expected to occupy.

## Religion
Religion can be an institution of conservative common sense, but it can also preserve moral languages of solidarity, sacrifice, and popular dignity. A Gramscian analysis asks how religious institutions are articulated with class forces, national traditions, family life, and political organization.

## Online Intellectuals
Influencers can behave as organic intellectuals for fractions of capital, professional-managerial strata, nationalist blocs, or emergent counter-publics. The test is not sincerity but function: do they organize common sense into durable political will, or do they only circulate identity, resentment, and consumption?

## Strategic Discipline
A Gramscian answer should usually move through four steps:
1. Name the relation of forces.
2. Identify the institutions producing consent and coercion.
3. Find the common sense and the good sense within it.
4. Ask what organization could turn scattered good sense into collective will.
