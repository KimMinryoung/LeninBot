# Gramsci Biographical and Historical Notes

## Sardinia and Formation
Antonio Gramsci was born in Sardinia in 1891. Sardinian poverty, regional subordination, and the contrast between northern industrial Italy and the southern/peripheral question shaped his sensitivity to uneven development. His Marxism was never only factory economics; it was also a theory of how regional, cultural, educational, and linguistic inequalities become political facts.

## Turin and Workers' Councils
In Turin, Gramsci encountered industrial workers, socialist politics, and the factory council movement. The Ordine Nuovo period treated councils as schools of working-class self-government, not merely workplace grievance bodies. For Gramsci, workers needed institutions that trained them to administer production and society.

## Communist Party and Fascism
Gramsci became a leader of the Italian Communist Party. The rise of fascism forced him to confront why socialist crisis did not automatically produce proletarian power. Fascism was not only police violence; it reorganized consent, mobilized petty-bourgeois fear, absorbed nationalism, disciplined labor, and exploited the failures of liberal and socialist leadership.

## Prison
Arrested by the fascist regime in 1926, Gramsci wrote the Prison Notebooks under censorship and illness. The fragmented style matters: concepts are often indirect, experimental, and coded. He avoided easy formulas because he was studying how politics works in societies where consent, culture, institutions, and coercion are interwoven.

## Reading Guidance
Do not reduce Gramsci to the slogan "cultural hegemony." He was a strategist of proletarian leadership under modern conditions. His central problem was how subordinate classes become capable of leading, not how intellectuals win arguments in culture alone.

## Relation to Lenin
Gramsci admired Lenin as a theorist of organization and hegemony. He interpreted Lenin's revolutionary practice as a form of political leadership that united class forces into a historical bloc. The difference is emphasis: in Western Europe, with stronger civil society, Gramsci thought revolutionary strategy required deeper war of position before decisive manoeuvre.

## Southern Question
The southern question concerned the alliance between northern workers and southern peasants, and the need to break the ideological and material blocs binding peasants to landlords, clergy, and conservative state forces. For modern analysis, it suggests that class strategy must grasp region, culture, land, migration, debt, language, and institutional dependency.
