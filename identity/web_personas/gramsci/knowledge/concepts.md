# Gramsci Concept Map

## Hegemony
Hegemony is not simple domination. It is leadership secured through a changing combination of consent and coercion. A ruling class becomes leading when its interests appear as the common sense of broader allied groups, not merely as the command of its own narrow economic fraction. Coercion remains present in the background, but durable rule depends on institutions that educate, habituate, reward, shame, and organize consent.

Analytical use:
- Ask which institutions translate class interests into common sense.
- Ask which groups are actively consenting, passively accepting, or being coerced.
- Distinguish ideological noise from organized leadership.
- Look for the material apparatus behind a worldview: schools, media, churches, unions, parties, courts, NGOs, platforms, foundations, professional associations.

## Civil Society and Political Society
Civil society is the terrain of associations, education, press, religion, culture, unions, parties, families, and professional organizations. Political society is the more direct apparatus of law, administration, police, courts, prisons, and military force. The modern state is an integral state: political society plus civil society. The boundary is analytical, not absolute.

Strategic warning: a movement that only confronts the government while ignoring civil society fights the visible fortress and misses the trench system around it.

## War of Manoeuvre and War of Position
War of manoeuvre is frontal assault, rapid crisis action, insurrectionary movement, or direct seizure of state power. War of position is long struggle through civil society, organization, culture, education, alliances, and patient construction of counter-hegemony.

Gramsci's point is not that manoeuvre is obsolete everywhere. The strategic form depends on the structure of the state and civil society. In societies with dense civil society, a frontal political victory can be absorbed unless a counter-hegemonic bloc has already been built.

## Organic Intellectuals
Every class that seeks leadership creates organic intellectuals: organizers, educators, technicians, writers, administrators, media figures, theorists, and cadres who articulate its experience into a worldview and a program. Organic intellectuals are not just academics. A union organizer, journalist, pastor, engineer, influencer, party cadre, or school administrator can perform this function.

Diagnostic question: who is translating lived economic position into language, institutions, and political direction?

## Traditional Intellectuals
Traditional intellectuals imagine themselves autonomous from class struggle because their institutions appear old, neutral, or universal. Clergy, professors, jurists, journalists, and cultural authorities often present themselves this way. The analytical task is not to insult them as puppets, but to locate the social relations that make their neutrality plausible and useful.

## Common Sense and Good Sense
Common sense is the contradictory everyday philosophy of a people: inherited religion, media assumptions, workplace lessons, national myths, practical wisdom, prejudices, fragments of scientific knowledge, and memories of struggle. Good sense is the critical, coherent element inside common sense that can be developed into conscious politics.

A serious movement does not sneer at common sense from outside. It works within it, separates good sense from passive inherited ideology, and reorganizes it.

## Passive Revolution
Passive revolution describes modernization or reform from above that absorbs pressures from below while preserving the initiative of dominant groups. It can grant reforms, reorganize the state, modernize production, or adopt the language of opposition while preventing subordinate classes from becoming independent historical actors.

Signs:
- reform without mass self-organization;
- elite adoption of radical language;
- institutional modernization that disorganizes the popular bloc;
- concessions that isolate militants from their social base.

## Caesarism
Caesarism appears when contending social forces block one another and a figure or apparatus claims to stand above them to restore order. It may be progressive or reactionary depending on the historical relation of forces, but it always indicates a crisis of hegemony and a failure of existing institutions to organize consent.

## Modern Prince
The modern Prince is the revolutionary party or collective political organizer capable of forming a national-popular will. It is not a mere election machine, propaganda office, or conspiratorial sect. It educates, coordinates, disciplines, listens, and builds the bridge between scattered grievances and historical agency.

## National-Popular
A socialist project must become national-popular without becoming nationalist in the chauvinist sense. It must speak the language of the people, absorb their historical experiences, and organize a collective will that can lead society. Abstract doctrine that never becomes national-popular remains a scholastic exercise.
